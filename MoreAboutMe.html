My name is Geno Rogers. Here is the little information about me.

Since becoming most completely paralyzed,

  • I have traveled in 44 countries and island nations, on six continents, and sailed on several oceans.
  • I have enjoyed diverse recreational activities ranging from skydiving and paragliding to scuba diving to trekking in the Himalayas - including an expedition to Mount Everest Base Camp.
  • I was, at one time, an entrepreneur and self-made millionaire.
  • I earned a bachelors degree in Education, an Masters of Business Administration, and graduate and post-graduate studies in rehabilitation.
  • I have worked in the field of disabilities, in Texas, New Mexico, and California.
  • I earned a Switzer Research Fellowship from the Federal Dept. of Education.
  • I became, and am still a TV producer.

Geno with his doggy Cheyenne

Whipps Ledges in Ohio

That’s me and my doggy Cheyenne. I’m the one on the right. Though I live in Austin, TX now, I grew up in Cleveland, Ohio.

I can speak English, Español languages.

I broke my neck at age 17 when I fell from a cliff. It was at Whipps Ledges in Ohio. The good news is, since then, I have had many adventures in 44 countries. I’ve gone sailing, sky diving, scuba diving, paragliding, trekking in the mountains, enjoyed many more adventure sports and even made an expedition to Mt. Everest Base Camp.

When I had my accident, there was no alcohol or drugs involved. I want to make that clear. I just happened to be in the cliff area, on a picnic, with high school friends. My last memory is walking towards the cliffs. The next thing I remember is waking up in the hospital 2 weeks later. I broke my neck at the C-5 level causing instant paralysis from the shoulders down. There was no law suit or insurance settlement. Thankfully my father was a good provider and had great insurance. Still, my poor parents were saddled with tremendous medical bills.

I wish I could say my 11 month stay in a rehab center helped but the fact is, the only thing I learned there was that I would have to take charge of my life. I credit my parents and many friends to my successful rehabilitation.

My parents were hard working and taught me the habits of industry at an early age. They told me I could have anything I wanted, as long as I got a job to pay for it. That still applied after I broke my neck. They set an example by working hard and rarely complaining. If something needed to be done, they did it rather than make excuses why they couldn’t. That was a valuable life lesson for me, my older brother and my younger five siblings. I credit my mother for being a “stay at home” mom, and my father for always having several “irons in the fire” at any one time.

I finished high school while in the rehab center. I earned my first college degree at Kent State, in Ohio, where I made friends that changed my life. That’s where my destiny was first shaped, where I developed the love for traveling and realized there really could be much more to life. It’s been 40+ years and I still stay in contact with those friends who became family.

My incredible friends helped me build an incredible life. They were mostly over achievers, high achievers, or simply people I admired, who all knew more than me. Since I only learn from people who are smarter or more talented than me, those are the people with whom I chose to associate.

After college I moved to New Mexico to get away from the snow and find a job. I was invited to join the Santa Fe Jaycees – a leadership training organization. They taught me about goal setting and giving back to the community. After working a few years at an ILC in Santa Fe, I moved to Albuquerque to get my MBA. Because of my disability, I was allowed extra time to take tests but I knew if I wanted to be successful, I would have to compete with my able-body peers. To that end, I took all tests and completed all projects in the same time as fellow students.

I created several assistive technologies that changed my life. Though they seem quite simple, they made a huge impact in my academic and vocational life.

I created a portable notebook-desk. I started with a standard notepad, then Velcro-ed my HP-12C calculator as well as my mini-computer that I programmed in Basic. I pushed buttons with a mouth-stick and wrote with a unique writing brace I made with the help of a clever occupational therapist. I never would have got a good job or my MBA without it. It had just 1 Velcro strap and I was able to put it on and take it off independently. That was a real game changer.

Goal setting was instrumental in much of my growth, particularly in education and traveling. By setting goals of getting my MBA by age 30 or going to X amount of countries or continents, it was easier to make plans. A goal without a plan is just a dream. As James Dean said, “Plan as if you’ll live forever; live as though you’ll die tomorrow.”

After working in New Mexico for several years I ended up in Austin, Texas where I live and work today. Austin is a mecca for people with disabilities. The weather here is beautiful year round and I made friends with folks in ADAPT, a disability rights organization. They too played a major role in my growth. ADAPT is a disability rights group that taught me the importance of asserting our civil rights. They taught me about individual responsibility in group processes. Participation in ADAPT actions really liberated my thinking.

I became an entrepreneur, and, after a time, a self-made millionaire. When I recognized the economy was changing, I jumped at the opportunity to take advantage of it. I bought and sold securities, leveraging my investments to the hilt. I parlayed my meager earnings into a tidy sum while using some of it to travel. Getting my MBA, I learned how to make a million dollars, I just didn’t learn how to keep it. That was a unique time in our history and I doubt we’ll ever see again. When the economy changed, I didn’t change with it quick enough. I lost most of my assets, including my fully accessible dream house I was having built. Suffice it to say, success favors the prepared mind. Experience, education and a lot of hard work had helped me build a business. I just wasn’t good at keeping the fruits of my labor.

I rebuilt my life – again. While designing a business card, I had an epiphany. I’ve had various job titles but I wanted a title that would never change. After all, my passions have remained constant. I realized that the only constant in my life is that I’m an Argonaut. I enjoy traveling, adventure and exploring as chronicled on my web page www.GenosPlace.org . There, you will find many pictures and a few videos of some of my adventures in 44 countries on 6 continents. From paragliding over the Swiss Alps to scuba diving in the Red Sea, I’ve enjoyed it all. I shared them on DVD and produced a show on public access TV called Geno’s Place.

In order to make my experiences more relevant, I created a new show called "The Gene And Dave" Show. Now, along with my co-host, Dave Dauber, create shows that are broadcast on Channel Austin. Our shows are captioned and archived at www.TheGeneAndDaveShow.com

Looking back on my life, I have concluded that my station in life is due to my upbringing, friends and family, hard work, and both good and bad luck. I’ve learned "Life is the sum of all your choices." "People believe they make choices, but it may be more accurate to say that their choices make them." In the words of J. K. Rowling, "It is our choices that show what we truly are, far more than our abilities."

I’m available for public speaking.
